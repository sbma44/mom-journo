Ordaal House
May 25 2022

Although today was forecasted to be rainy, it started out with beautiful sun! \240Around here, you have to always dress in layers, have your raincoat handy, and be able to fend off the wind. \240You can put your laundry out on the line so long as you’re home to take it in!

One of the friendly neighbors, Mary, walked with me a ways as I was headed to the bus stop, having been advised that the 10:30 bus could take me to the Unst Boat Haven Museum. \240She chided me for not being more aware of the many beautiful wayside wild “flooers,” and suggested I buy a book at a local unadvertised shop, which she pointed out to me. \240I think I’ll go there tomorrow. \240It has multiple chimneys, easy to find.

I set out to visit the Unst Boat Haven. \240During the time of our American Civil War (mid-1800’s), Baltasound was a fishing mecca, and thousands of fishing people swelled the population here all summer long. \240I suspect the preponderance of derelict stone buildings is due to these days of old. \240It’s so hard to live here and do well: it’s just so far from the mainland, and everything has to be brought over on the ferry—except rocks! \240

I learned a great deal while in this museum, and found it way more interesting than expected. \240This is a real coracle!

This type of Berchon collapsible boat became more useful after the sinking of the Titanic. \240Before then, its inventor had trouble getting a patent.

I saw the progress from buoys made of wood, which were based on a Dutch design, to those made of sheep or cattle bladders drenched in oils. \240One buoy was made out of dog skin, and was reputedly the best, strongest skin for such a buoy. \240The exhibit described the awful way a fisherman would feed up his dog and then kill it. \240This fell out of favor, thankfully. It was a horror to learn that this cruelty was inflicted on man’s best friend.

Baltasound, Unst, became the herring capital of the world, and the fish here were prized for their flavor and heft. \240The population here in Baltasound grew to 6000 each year gathering the herring. Many of the boats were modeled on Dutch and Norse models, but evolved into “sixareens,” or six-oared. \240The museum had many beautiful model boats, and also real, famous boats from this area’s fishing heyday. \240It makes me wonder whether this area got all fished out, with these fourareens and etc. \240

The Norse (or Vikings) call Shetland “Hjaltland.”

A good view of the very interesting Unst Boat Haven Museum. \240I didn’t think I’d be as interested as I became. \240One thing is certain: the life of a seafaring fisherman is very dangerous. \240Many times gales would sudden come up, the men would lose control and the boats would capsize. \240Drownings were common. \240Courage was not wanting in these men, nor in the women they loved.

These are a band of \240“gutter lasses.” \240They were quite happy to be gutting the fish, and placing them in these barrels, all made by hand. \240The alternative for them would be to become nannies, nurses, or teachers. \240Traveling with the fish was a bit like following a harvest to different areas of the land, and a more adventurous life for many young women. \240I think I’d also have chosen the life of a “gutter lass.” \240See how happy and full of good cheer they appear in this photo!

Making all the barrels by hand was certainly a laborious craft. \240There were numerous men who spent their entire careers as coopersmiths. \240Equally important, the crafts of boat building, net making, ironworking, waterproofing with tar, and timely repairs on all of the above.

A Sixareen. \240The origin and history of each of the boats in the museum was cited on paper. \240A boat is always a “she,” and many had names like “Margaret.” \240She was, in fact, one of the most famous boats here, and won prizes in racing.

I learned that the clothing fishermen wore was made of cotton which was boiled a few times in linseed oil, and after being treated this way, again painted once with it. That made it quite pliable, yet waterproof. \240This reminded me of the first treatment for a new saddle or bridle. You don’t want to use linseed oil more than once; once is sufficient.

What a whalebone! \240As you walk along in Unst, you see shells everywhere, especially mussel shells. \240In the middle of the sound today, there are mussel farms. \240You can see the nets in the distance. \240Salmon are also farmed. \240Yesterday I saw some smoked mackerel that looked really delicious. \240I think I’ll have to buy it today. \240It would be a shame not to have at least a bit of Shetland fish while here! \240There are some hotels which probably have restaurants, but I really haven’t seen a single one advertised! \240Not one restaurant except for the tearoom. \240No fish there, obviously!

This was the most beautiful tableau of shells that I’ve ever seen, collected and labeled in a gorgeous case.

Times have changed so dramatically here in Unst from its heyday all about the fishing industry. \240I would have liked to have asked questions of the man minding the Boat Haven Museum, but he told me he wasn’t a sailor. \240I heard him talking, later on when I returned briefly to use the bathroom, to a couple about his bypass surgery. \240I do think the lack of vegetables here must cause the diet of most people to be poor. \240Oats and grains are fine, and I did buy a really great tangerine, but all the food is expensive. \240Inflation is everywhere, even here.

A barometer was consulted before sailing.

And here is my friend and fellow harper, Sunita Staneslow’s pied-a-terre in Unst! \240Neither she nor her husband, Fred, were there, but I had a good look at what they’ve been up to, rehabilitating an ancient stone house. \240What a huge labor of love! \240

Here you can see what they’ve done thus far. \240I looked inside: it’s not yet habitable, but soon! \240I saw a poster they’d made up promising to feed (well) anyone who’d come and help! \240On Facebook, they’ve put up a video about their plans for this place to become a holiday rental, with arts and crafts for sale downstairs. \240I peered inside, and it’s a great cavern thus far. \240The roof is very sound, which they said, along with repairing and reinforcing the exterior walls, was imperative to attend to initially.

I did stop at one very successful business in Unst: Victoria’s Vintage Tearooms and Gift Shop. \240I had her carrot & coriander soup with her cheese scone, followed by cafe Americanos and a (huge) slice of Victoria’s sponge cake. \240Yum! \240Not going to say no until I’m home again.

Marsh marigold and Field Gentian! \240Thank you, Mary!

View of Baltasound where the wind blows in. \240Whereas Ordaal House, where I’m staying, is on the east end, Sunita’s place is facing the wind, right on the water, and I should think will be far colder and wetter.

Eyebright, once used as a remedy for poor eyesight. \240The cool, damp climate of Shetland has favored peat formation. I saw much peat at Hermaness, and here as well. \240In the old days, they called it “flittin’ da peats.” \240They made strong baskets out of straw which they filled up with peat, then placed the baskets on top of straw blankets on the ponies. \240The women did much of this part, then brought the baskets to the boats, and the men would load up these baskets to take them wherever they could sell them. \240They either rowed or sailed, according to the weather.

This page from “Flittin Peats in Fetlar” shows a slice of a life gone by, as well as some of the local dialect of Shetland. \240It’s definitely hard to understand!

I sense God’s presence when I walk along Baltasound. The waves lapping and the birds calling, it’s a wonder!

The seaweed is so slippery, but also bulbous, as I remember from Long Island Sound as a child, when I could “pop it” between my fingertips, and here I can as well!

Inside the Viking Longhouse replica.

From the outside. This reminds me of the turf huts in Iceland, but unlike them, it’s really large inside.

A Viking boat replica. \240Shallow, it could come easily ashore.

I love this double stone wall. Who could have built these walls? \240How long ago?

Shetland ponies everywhere here…I tried walking to the Hagdale Horse Mill, but thought I’d miss my bus back, so when I’d turned around, I saw a most magnificent sight: 5 ponies started bucking and galloping happily down the hillside, then rolling. \240Just a joyful sight! \240I got a video of them, simply heartbreakingly beautiful!!

9 pm at Ordaal House.
